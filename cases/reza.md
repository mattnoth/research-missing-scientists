## Narrative

### Background

Monica Jacinto Reza is an American metallurgist and materials engineer of Hispanic descent. She began her career at Rocketdyne in 1988, working on oxygen-rich staged combustion cycle engines. In the mid-1990s, she and Australian metallurgist Dallis Hardwick (1950-2015) developed Mondaloy, a nickel-based superalloy that withstands high-pressure, high-temperature gaseous oxygen without combusting and without requiring protective coatings. The name "Mondaloy" is a portmanteau in which "Mon" derives from Monica. The alloy was eventually used in approximately 12 components of Aerojet Rocketdyne's AR1 engine and the Hydrocarbon Boost Technology Demonstrator.

Reza spent over 37 years at Aerojet Rocketdyne, achieving the rank of Technical Fellow (the company's highest technical rank). She subsequently became Director of the Materials Processing Group at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena, California. Her work had direct relevance to U.S. rocket engine development, including efforts to reduce dependence on Russian-made RD-180 engines.

### Disappearance

On the morning of June 22, 2025, Reza went hiking on the Mount Waterman Trail in the Angeles National Forest with two companions from her yoga group (one male, one female). She did not drive herself; her car was parked at a companion's residence. At approximately 9:10 a.m., a hiking companion approximately 30 feet ahead on the trail turned to check on her. Reza smiled and waved. When the companion looked back moments later, she was gone. One source reports that Reza and her male companion had been running on the steep, uneven terrain, which was described as unusual.

Reza was last seen wearing a red long-sleeved shirt, green hiking pants, and hiking shoes. She reportedly carried a backpack with several liters of water. She is described as a white female, 4'11", 101 lbs, with gray hair and brown eyes.

### Search Efforts

LASD initiated an extensive search involving Los Angeles County Search and Rescue teams, aerial resources (helicopters), FLIR thermal imaging, canine units, forensic technology, and state and federal assets. Acting Captain Ryan A. Vienna of LASD Crescenta Valley Station described it as an "extraordinary search." Physical evidence recovered was limited to one beanie and one lip balm, which were described as inconsistent with her expected trajectory. Scent dogs reportedly terminated at the beanie in a ravine. The initial response phase concluded on June 29, 2025, and the case was transferred to the Homicide Bureau Missing Persons Unit.

Volunteer searches continued monthly through at least November 2025 across an area described as a 40-50 mile radius. No remains, additional personal effects, or definitive evidence of her fate have been found.

### Cell Phone Evidence

Montrose Search and Rescue acknowledged in a since-removed Facebook post that they "worked closely with technical experts to explore cell phone forensic data." The results of this analysis have never been publicly released. Separately, a 911 call from the same morning and general area reported a woman screaming, which establishes that cellular connectivity existed on the ridge that morning. Whether Reza carried a phone and what her device's last ping showed remain officially undisclosed.

### Federal Investigation

Reza's disappearance is one of 11 cases of missing or deceased scientists with government/defense connections under review by the Trump White House, the FBI, and the House Oversight Committee. Chairman James Comer (R-KY) and Subcommittee Chairman Eric Burlison (R-MO) sent letters to the DOE, DOD, FBI, and NASA in April 2026. White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt confirmed the probe, stating "no stone will be unturned."

## Contradictions and Anomalies

1. **Find a Grave memorial created while search was active.** A memorial listing her death date as June 22, 2025, and marking a "green burial" appeared on June 26, four days after disappearance and three days before LASD concluded its initial search phase. No death certificate, obituary, or funeral announcement has surfaced. The memorial was removed after media attention in March 2026.

2. **No remains despite extensive search.** Multi-week professional search with thermal imaging, canines, aerial assets, and hundreds of volunteers across a large area found only a beanie and lip balm. The terrain is rugged but well-trafficked.

3. **Cell phone data obtained but not released.** LASD/SAR acknowledged obtaining cell phone forensic data, then removed the post. No official statement has addressed her phone's last known location or status.

4. **911 call about screaming.** An unidentified caller reported hearing a woman screaming in the Mt. Waterman area on the morning of her disappearance. This has not been publicly connected to or excluded from her case by LASD.

5. **Running on steep terrain.** One source reports she and her male companion were running on steep, uneven ground before she vanished, which was described as atypical.

6. **Title discrepancy (minor).** Sources variously describe her JPL role as "Director of Materials Processing," "Director of the Materials Processing Group," and "Fellow-Materials Science and Engineering at Aerojet Rocketdyne." The JPL directorship and the Rocketdyne fellowship may be sequential roles rather than contradictory, but no JPL directory or organizational chart has been located to confirm.

7. **Transportation dependency.** Reza did not drive herself to the trailhead and her vehicle was at a companion's home, meaning she was dependent on companions for departure.

## Open Questions

1. **What did cell phone forensic data show?** Where was Reza's phone's last ping? Was the phone recovered? What carrier and device?
2. **Who created the Find a Grave memorial and on what basis?** "Lillian" and "J.C." have not been publicly identified. Was there a death certificate filed?
3. **What was the 911 caller's exact report?** Time, duration, location precision? Has LASD connected it to Reza's case?
4. **What are the identities and accounts of the two hiking companions?** Their names have not appeared in public reporting. What did each observe?
5. **Was Reza still employed at JPL at the time, or had she recently transitioned from Aerojet Rocketdyne?** Her 37+ years at Rocketdyne and presence at JPL in 2025 need timeline clarification.
6. **Did Reza hold an active security clearance?** Reported but not officially confirmed with clearance level.
7. **Has any federal agency (FBI, NASA) issued a formal statement specifically about Reza's case?**
8. **Is there physical evidence beyond the beanie and lip balm?** Were these items forensically tested?
9. **What was the professional connection, if any, between Reza and Maj. Gen. William McCasland?** Both had ties to Wright-Patterson AFB-adjacent work.
10. **Has Dallis Hardwick's death (2015) been examined in connection with this pattern?** She was the co-inventor of Mondaloy.

## Verification of Key Claims

### "Co-inventor of a nickel superalloy for rocket engines" -- CONFIRMED

Three US patent applications (US-20030053926-A1, US-20040208777-A1, US-20100266442-A1) list Monica A. Jacinto and Dallis Ann Hardwick as co-inventors of "Burn-resistant and high tensile strength metal alloys." The alloy, commercially known as Mondaloy (variants Mondaloy 100 and Mondaloy 200), is a nickel-based superalloy designed for rocket engine components operating in high-pressure, oxygen-rich environments. Used in approximately 12 components of the AR1 engine and HBTD program.

### "Director of Materials Processing at NASA JPL" -- PARTIALLY VERIFIED

The LASD missing person listing on Solve the Case states she is "Director of the Materials Processing Group at NASA JPL." This is a law-enforcement-sourced record (T1), but no JPL organizational directory or NASA statement has been located to independently confirm the exact title. Wikipedia (with reliability caveats noted on the page itself) and multiple T2 news sources repeat the title. The claim is assessed as **Reported** with high confidence.
