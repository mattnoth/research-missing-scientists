# Amy Catherine Eskridge

**Status:** Deceased
**Date of incident:** June 11, 2022
**Location:** Huntsville, Alabama (her home)
**Affiliation:** Co-founder, Chairwoman, and President of The Institute for Exotic Science; CEO and co-founder of HoloChron LLC (with father Richard Eskridge, retired NASA engineer)
**Inclusion rationale:** Eskridge researched gravity-modification / "antigravity" propulsion technology and co-founded organizations with her father, a retired NASA Marshall Space Flight Center engineer who specialized in plasma physics and fusion technology. Her work overlapped with advanced aerospace propulsion concepts, giving her an indirect but substantive connection to the aerospace/defense research ecosystem. She publicly claimed threats against her life related to her research before her death. However, this is a **weaker fit** for several reasons: (1) her death occurred in June 2022, predating the 2025-2026 cluster by three years; (2) her organizations were private, not government-funded or classified; (3) the official ruling is suicide by self-inflicted gunshot wound; (4) her father has publicly stated there was "nothing suspicious" about her death; (5) no police or coroner reports have been publicly released, but no official source has disputed the suicide ruling. The case is included because it has been retroactively aggregated into the broader narrative by media and independent investigators, and because her pre-death statements about threats are a matter of public record that warrant documentation.

## Narrative of Known Facts

Amy Catherine Eskridge was born September 19, 1987, in Huntsville, Alabama, to Kathy and Richard Eskridge [T1 (obituary), Confirmed]. She graduated from the University of Alabama in Huntsville with a double major in chemistry and biology and pursued additional expertise across electrical engineering, chemistry, physics, and genetic engineering [T1 (obituary), Confirmed]. Her obituary describes her as having "a genius for science" who "questioned the conventional wisdom on everything from the universe to the nanoparticles of the atom" [T1 (obituary), Confirmed].

Eskridge co-founded The Institute for Exotic Science in Huntsville, serving as its chairwoman and president [T1 (obituary), T2 (Newsweek, Fox News), Confirmed]. The Institute focused on research in gravity modification, quantum computing, metamaterial science, and related advanced technologies [T2 (multiple sources), Reported]. She also co-founded HoloChron LLC, described as a father-daughter company focused on gravity modification research and development, where her father Richard Eskridge served as Chief Technology Officer [T2, Reported].

Her father, Richard Eskridge, was a retired engineer and scientist at NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center in Huntsville, where he contributed to research including pulsed inductive thrusters and other propulsion-related studies, specializing in plasma physics and fusion technology [T2 (Newsweek, Fox News, multiple sources), Reported].

In December 2018, Eskridge presented "A Historical Perspective on Anti-Gravity Technology" at a meeting of HAL5 (Huntsville Alabama L5 Society), a public space advocacy group [T1 (HAL5 PDF record), Confirmed].

In a recorded interview on May 20, 2020, with YouTuber Jeremy Rys and researcher Mark Sokol, Eskridge made several notable claims [T2 (published interview), Confirmed as statements made; claims unverified]:
- She stated: "We discovered antigravity, and our lives went to [expletive] and people started sabotaging us. It's harassment, threats. It's awful."
- She described threats as becoming "more and more aggressive," including alleged incidents involving a "directed energy weapon" that caused burns on her body.
- She explained her rationale for public disclosure: "If you stick your neck out in public, at least someone knows if your head gets chopped off." She contrasted this with private disclosure: "If you stick your neck out in private, they will bury you. They will burn down your house while you're sleeping in your bed, and it won't even make the news."
- She stated she co-founded the Institute for Exotic Science "as a public-facing persona to disclose antigravity technology."
- She described observing other scientists who reported breakthroughs "disappear" from public work or stop publishing, suggesting institutional pressure.

Amy Eskridge died on June 11, 2022, at age 34, in Huntsville, Alabama [T1 (obituary), Confirmed]. Her death has been reported as a self-inflicted gunshot wound to the head [T2 (Fox News, Newsweek, multiple outlets), Reported]. However, neither the Huntsville Police Department nor the Madison County Coroner has publicly released an investigative report or detailed findings [gap]. No police incident report specific to her death has been located in public records searches.

Her obituary, published June 21, 2022, in AL.com (Huntsville) and The Arab Tribune, does not specify the cause of death [T1 (obituary), Confirmed]. She is survived by her parents Kathy and Richard Eskridge, brothers Matt and Michael Eskridge, and sister-in-law Victoria Eskridge [T1 (obituary), Confirmed].

After Eskridge's death, the Institute for Exotic Science's website became inaccessible [T2 (Newsweek), Reported].

In April 2026, Eskridge's case was identified as the "11th" in a growing media list of scientists with ties to defense, aerospace, or advanced research who had died or gone missing under circumstances deemed unusual. Her father Richard Eskridge publicly rejected conspiracy theories, telling NewsNation there was "nothing suspicious" about his daughter's death and stating: "Scientists die also, just like other people" [T2 (NewsNation), Reported].

Separately, retired British intelligence officer Franc Milburn has claimed to have investigated Eskridge's case and concluded her death was not a suicide [T3/T5 (media reports of Milburn's claims), Alleged]. Milburn reportedly stated on the radio program "Coast to Coast AM" that "somebody was after her work" [T3 (media reporting of radio appearance), Alleged]. It has been reported that Milburn's findings were submitted to Congress by independent investigators in 2023, but no congressional record of this submission has been located [T3/T5, Alleged]. Journalist Michael Shellenberger reportedly testified during a UAP hearing that Eskridge was "murdered by a 'private aerospace company,'" but the evidentiary basis for this claim has not been publicly documented [T3/T5, Alleged].

## What Is Documented vs. Reported vs. Alleged vs. Speculated

### Documented (Tier 1)
- Obituary confirms: born September 19, 1987; died June 11, 2022; parents Kathy and Richard Eskridge; graduated UAH with chemistry and biology double major; co-founded Institute for Exotic Science; survived by parents and brothers.
- HAL5 presentation record (December 2018 PDF) confirms she publicly presented on antigravity topics.
- The May 20, 2020 interview with Jeremy Rys is a matter of public record (published video). The statements she made are confirmed as having been made; the underlying claims (threats, DEW attacks, discovery of antigravity) are unverified.

### Reported (Tier 2)
- Death reported as self-inflicted gunshot wound by multiple news outlets (Fox News, Newsweek, NewsNation), though the original source for this characterization is unclear.
- Father Richard Eskridge told NewsNation there was "nothing suspicious" about her death and dismissed conspiracy theories.
- Father Richard Eskridge was a retired NASA Marshall Space Flight Center engineer specializing in plasma physics and fusion technology.
- HoloChron LLC described as a joint venture between Amy and Richard Eskridge focused on gravity modification R&D.
- Institute for Exotic Science website went offline after her death.

### Alleged
- Franc Milburn (retired British intelligence officer) claims to have investigated the case and concluded it was not suicide. Milburn's credentials are reported but not independently verified in this research. His findings were reportedly submitted to Congress in 2023, but no congressional record has been located.
- Michael Shellenberger reportedly testified at a UAP hearing that Eskridge was murdered by a "private aerospace company." The evidentiary basis is not publicly documented.
- Eskridge herself alleged threats, harassment, surveillance, break-ins, and directed energy weapon attacks in her 2020 interview. These claims were made on a YouTube podcast and have not been corroborated by police reports, medical records, or independent witnesses.

### Speculated
- That Eskridge's death is connected to her antigravity research or to the 2025-2026 cluster of missing/dead scientists. No evidence links her 2022 death to any other case.
- That the Institute for Exotic Science's website going offline after her death indicates suppression. Alternative explanations (hosting lapsed, family took it down) have not been explored in reporting.
- That Eskridge possessed genuine antigravity technology breakthroughs. Her published presentations describe theoretical frameworks and historical surveys, not demonstrated experimental results.
- Social media narratives frame her as having been "silenced." This is speculative and contradicted by her father's public statement.

## Contradictions
1. **Father vs. external investigators:** Richard Eskridge explicitly stated there was "nothing suspicious" about his daughter's death and dismissed conspiracy theories. This directly contradicts Franc Milburn's conclusion that it was not suicide and Shellenberger's testimony alleging murder. The father, as the closest family member and a fellow scientist who worked alongside Amy, is a highly relevant source.
2. **Suicide ruling vs. absence of public documentation:** The death is widely reported as ruled a suicide by self-inflicted gunshot, but no police report, coroner's report, or medical examiner's finding has been publicly released. The source of the "suicide" characterization in media reporting is unclear -- it may come from family, police communications with media, or official records not yet made public.
3. **Threat claims vs. evidence:** Eskridge's 2020 claims of harassment, surveillance, break-ins, and directed energy weapon attacks are dramatic and specific but entirely self-reported on a YouTube podcast. No police reports, medical records documenting burns, or corroborating witnesses have been publicly identified.
4. **"Antigravity discovery" claims:** Eskridge stated in 2020 that she was preparing to publish "groundbreaking foundational research" on antigravity and needed NASA authorization. No such publication has appeared, and her HAL5 presentation was a historical survey, not a report of experimental results.
5. **Timeline disconnect:** Eskridge died in June 2022, three years before the 2025-2026 cluster of cases. Her inclusion in the pattern is retroactive, driven by media aggregation in April 2026 rather than by contemporaneous reporting or investigation.
